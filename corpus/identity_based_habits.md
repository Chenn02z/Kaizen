# Identity-Based Habits

Identity-based habits are built by shifting the story you tell yourself about who you are, rather than focusing only on what you want to achieve. Instead of "I want to run a 5K," you begin with "I am someone who moves their body every day." Your actions then become evidence for that identity rather than means to an external goal.

The psychology is that behavior follows belief. When your identity includes a trait — "I am a reader," "I am someone who does not eat junk food" — every related action either reinforces or threatens that self-concept. This makes the habit intrinsically motivated, not contingent on hitting a target.

When you are starting a new habit, notice the framing you use internally. "I am trying to go to the gym" creates distance — you are an outsider attempting to join a club. "I am someone who exercises" positions you as already a member; the workout is just what members do. Even when the evidence is thin — you have gone twice this week — claim the identity lightly and let the habits build the proof.

Each time you follow through, you cast a vote for that identity. Each time you skip, you cast a vote against it. Neither single vote is decisive, but over weeks the pattern determines whether you believe the identity or not. Use your logs to notice the votes adding up, and remind yourself of that accumulation when motivation is low.
