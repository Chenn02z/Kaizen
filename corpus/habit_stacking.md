# Habit Stacking

Habit stacking is the practice of attaching a new behavior to an existing one, using a reliable daily routine as an anchor. The formula is: "After I do [current habit], I will do [new habit]."

The technique works because existing habits are already encoded as strong neural pathways. By hooking a new behavior onto a stable cue, you leverage that existing momentum rather than building a trigger from scratch. The existing habit becomes the prompt for the new one.

When you want to add a daily reading practice, look for a habit that already happens consistently — brushing your teeth, making coffee, or sitting down after dinner. Then declare the link: "After I sit down with my evening tea, I will read for twenty minutes before turning on any screens." The specificity of the anchor habit is important; vague links like "after breakfast" work less reliably than "after I put my breakfast bowl in the sink."

You can stack multiple habits into a short sequence over time. Start with one pair until it feels automatic — typically a few weeks — then add the next. Building a morning routine this way (wake → stretch → meditate → journal) is more durable than trying to adopt all four at once, because each habit reinforces the next.
