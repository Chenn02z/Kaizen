# Two-Minute Rule

The two-minute rule states that when you want to build a new habit, you should start by doing a version of it that takes no longer than two minutes. The goal is not to complete the full habit every time — it is to show up consistently, even in the smallest possible form.

The underlying principle is that starting is the hardest part. Once you have crossed the threshold and begun, continuing becomes far easier. A two-minute version of a habit removes the psychological weight of committing to a long session, which is often what causes avoidance.

When you tell yourself you need to meditate for thirty minutes but feel resistant, the real barrier is the perceived cost of the full commitment. Instead, commit to just sitting down, closing your eyes, and taking five slow breaths — that is your two-minute version. On most days, you will keep going past two minutes. On hard days, even two minutes keeps the habit alive.

Apply this to exercise by committing to just putting on your workout clothes and stepping outside. For reading, it means opening the book and reading one page. The two-minute rule works because the identity of "someone who shows up daily" matters more than the duration of any single session. Consistency across days is worth more than perfect sessions on good days only.
