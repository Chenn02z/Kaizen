# Progress Tracking

Progress tracking is the practice of making your behavior visible through measurement — recording what you did, when you did it, and how it felt. The act of tracking creates a feedback loop that increases awareness, strengthens motivation, and surfaces patterns that are otherwise invisible.

The behavioral mechanism has two parts. First, observation changes behavior: when you know you are measuring something, you are more likely to show up (the Hawthorne effect applied to self-monitoring). Second, visible progress is intrinsically rewarding — the streak on a habit tracker, the logged kilometers, the filled-in checkboxes all provide small dopamine signals that reinforce continuation.

When you start tracking a habit, keep the format as frictionless as possible. A simple daily check — did I do it? yes/no — is more sustainable than a detailed log. A streak calendar (marking each successful day with an X) creates a visual chain you become reluctant to break. The goal of tracking is not to collect data for its own sake; it is to create a feedback signal that makes success visible and failure informative.

The most useful tracking includes a brief note about context when you miss: "skipped gym — exhausted after travel." Over weeks, these notes reveal patterns. If you notice you always miss on Thursdays, that is a signal to either plan around Thursdays or protect the habit more specifically on those days. Track not just what you did but what made it easy or hard.
